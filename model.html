Initial Exploration

Using decision trees, we could train our data and then test it out on selected training and testing sets. From this, we could find which statistics were most important in beating the spread.


Our data and analysis showed that passing and rushing stats were the most important. More specifically, being efficient with the plays a team had seemed to matter more than just having more possessions. Passing yards per attempt and rushing yards per attempt, for both offense and defense, were the major determinants in beating the line.

Where We Went From There


To predict outcomes of future games, we averaged past statistics overcmultiple games. These plots show that averaging over the previous 12 to 14 games is the best strategy.


A Model for Predicting Winners

As we were only interested in predicting the winner of a game instead of the margin of victory, there were many models available to us. We tried Random Forests, k-Nearest Neighbors, and Support Vector Machines. We finally tried an ensemble prediction based on the majority of those three models.

We expected that the Vegas lines would be more accurate than our models because the betting market can use more factors, such as injury data. In testing this, we found that our expectation was correct.

The Vegas line appears to always have a lower RMSE than the linear regression model.


Predicting Final Scores

To predict final scores of games, we needed a new model, and linear regression seemed like the best bet. Just like before, we conducted a preliminary regression analysis to determine which variables might be important. From the regression analysis, we found that all of the variables are significant except for interceptions. Since interceptions has a small coefficient, it wouldn't hurt the model to leave it in. We expected our models to perform worse on the test sets than the validation sets. However, the RMSE scores on the test sets appear to be just as close to the Las Vegas RMSE scores as on the validation sets. The prediction accuracy also held steady.
